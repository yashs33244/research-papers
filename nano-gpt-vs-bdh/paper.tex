\begin{abstract}
The Dragon Hatchling (BDH) is a recent brain-inspired language-model
architecture that replaces the Transformer's softmax attention and dense MLP
with linear attention over a high-dimensional, positive, sparse neuron field
\cite{kosowski2025bdh}. I reimplement both a GPT-2-style Transformer and BDH
from scratch and train them identically on character-level TinyShakespeare. At
their natural sizes BDH carries roughly twice the Transformer's parameters
(\BdhBigParamsM{}M vs \GptParamsM{}M) and wins every metric, sweeping a
position-controlled Claude judge \JudgeBigScore{} over \NPrompts{} prompts
($p=\PvalBig$); but a larger model almost always writes better, so that
comparison is confounded by size. This paper controls for it. I shrink BDH's
neuron multiplier so both models have
matched parameter budgets (\GptParamsK{}K for GPT, \BdhParamsK{}K for BDH,
matched within \MatchPct{}), retrain both from scratch under identical settings, and
rerun the full harness: held-out perplexity over \NWindows{} windows, five
automatic text-quality metrics, and the swap-controlled judge. The advantage survives. At matched size BDH again
wins the head-to-head: perplexity \BdhPpl{} vs \GptPpl{}, real-word rate
\BdhRealWord{} vs \GptRealWord{}, best validation loss \BdhValLoss{} vs
\GptValLoss{}, bigram diversity a tie, and a
\MatchScore{} decisive judge sweep at \Consistency{} swap-consistency
($p=\Pval$). Strikingly, halving BDH's parameters barely moved its perplexity
(\BdhPplBig{} to \BdhPpl{}), so it was heavily over-parameterized at 2$\times$ and
lost almost nothing on the way down. On this task, at equal size, the win is
architectural, not a size effect.
The broader lesson is methodological: a two-times parameter gap alone can produce
a clean-looking \JudgeBigScore{} sweep with $p<10^{-8}$, so only a
parameter-matched comparison separates architecture from size. All code, raw model
responses, judge verdicts, and the analysis pipeline that emits every number in
this paper are released.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Nearly every large language model in production is a Transformer
\cite{vaswani2017attention}: softmax self-attention mixes information across
positions, and a dense feed-forward MLP processes each position. The Dragon
Hatchling (BDH) \cite{kosowski2025bdh} is a recent and deliberately different
proposal, motivated by models of the brain. Instead of a compact residual
stream, BDH lifts each token into a much larger space of ``neurons,'' applies a
ReLU so activations are non-negative and mostly zero (sparse), mixes information
with \emph{linear} attention \cite{katharopoulos2020linear} rather than softmax,
and gates two sparse codes multiplicatively in place of the MLP. The authors
argue this makes the model more interpretable and biologically plausible while
remaining competitive with the Transformer.

Claims like ``competitive with the Transformer'' are exactly the kind a small,
controlled, from-scratch reimplementation can test. I built both architectures
from first principles (heavily commented, teaching-oriented code) and trained
them under one shared harness on character-level TinyShakespeare, the standard
minimal setting for studies of this kind \cite{karpathy2022nanogpt}.

\paragraph{Size versus architecture.} Comparing a new architecture against a
Transformer is easy to get wrong. Left at their natural teaching defaults, the
BDH model carries roughly twice the parameters of the Transformer
(\BdhBigParamsM{}M vs \GptParamsM{}M), and at that size it leads on \emph{every}
axis: lower perplexity, more real English words, and a \JudgeBigScore{} sweep of
a position-controlled LLM judge with a binomial $p$-value of $\PvalBig$. But
larger language models almost always write better, so a comparison at unequal
sizes cannot separate architecture from scale; the size has to be held fixed
before any of this counts as an architectural result.

\paragraph{This paper.} I ask one question and answer it cleanly: \emph{does
BDH's advantage survive parameter matching?} Because BDH's parameter count is
dominated by three shared matrices that scale as the neuron multiplier times the
embedding width squared, halving the multiplier (32 to 16) brings BDH from
\BdhBigParamsK{}K down to \BdhParamsK{}K parameters, matched to within
\MatchPct{} of the Transformer's \GptParamsK{}K, so neither model has a material
size advantage (Section~\ref{sec:method} details the counting convention). I
retrain both models from scratch under byte-for-byte identical
training settings and rerun the entire evaluation harness. Contributions:

\begin{itemize}
\item A clean, from-scratch reimplementation of both a GPT-2-style Transformer
  and BDH sharing one training and evaluation harness, so the only variable
  between them is the architecture.
\item A parameter-matched head-to-head at \GptParamsK{}K/\BdhParamsK{}K
  parameters on three independent metric families (perplexity, automatic
  text-quality metrics, and a position-bias-controlled LLM judge), with the
  earlier two-times-size result reported alongside as the motivating confound.
\item A partial reproduction of BDH's headline interpretability property
  (activation sparsity) at small scale, reported honestly against the paper's
  large-scale figure.
\item The full harness and raw data: model code, both sets of checkpoints, all
  generated completions, both orderings of every judge verdict, and the script
  that emits every number and table here.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

The Transformer \cite{vaswani2017attention} and its decoder-only, autoregressive
form popularized by GPT-2 \cite{radford2019gpt2} are the baseline this paper
compares against; the concrete implementation follows the minimal nanoGPT
formulation \cite{karpathy2022nanogpt}. BDH \cite{kosowski2025bdh} is the system
under test: it combines linear attention \cite{katharopoulos2020linear}, rotary
position embeddings \cite{su2021roformer}, and a high-dimensional sparse neuron
field with multiplicative gating, and argues that positivity and sparsity of
activations yield monosemantic, interpretable units in the spirit of dictionary
-learning interpretability \cite{bricken2023monosemanticity}. My contribution is
not a new architecture but a controlled, honest measurement: prior informal
comparisons of new architectures against Transformers are frequently confounded
by parameter count, and this paper is a small worked example of isolating
architecture from size, together with a faithful (if small-scale) reproduction
of BDH's sparsity claim.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\paragraph{Models.} Both models are reimplemented from scratch with parallel
interfaces so a single training loop drives either one. The Transformer is the
standard pre-norm, decoder-only GPT-2 arrangement: learned token and position
embeddings, causal multi-head softmax self-attention, a $4\times$ GELU MLP,
residual connections, and weight-tied output projection. BDH follows the
official reference implementation \cite{kosowski2025bdh}: per layer it lifts the
$C$-dimensional state into a neuron space of $N$ neurons per head via a shared
encoder, applies a ReLU (positive, sparse), mixes positions with causal linear
attention using rotary embeddings and a strictly-lower-triangular mask, forms a
second sparse code from the attention output, gates the two codes elementwise,
and projects back to $C$ with a shared decoder. Unlike the Transformer, BDH
reuses one set of encoder/decoder matrices at every layer (depth-wise weight
sharing) and uses affine-free LayerNorm and no learned position table.

\paragraph{Parameter matching.} BDH's parameter count is dominated by three
shared matrices (two encoders and one decoder), each of size proportional to
$m\,C^2$, where $m$ is the neuron multiplier ($N=mC/\text{heads}$) and $C$ is the
embedding width; the head count cancels. With $C=128$ and $m=32$, BDH has
\BdhBigParamsK{}K parameters against the Transformer's \GptParamsK{}K, a
$\sim$2$\times$ gap. Halving the multiplier to $m=16$ (so $N=512$ neurons per
head, still a genuinely high-dimensional field) gives \BdhParamsK{}K
parameters, within \MatchPct{} of the Transformer. All counts are total
trainable parameters. The two conventions agree that neither model has a
material size edge: by total parameters BDH (\BdhParamsK{}K) is marginally
smaller than GPT (\GptParamsK{}K), because GPT's total includes a
16{,}384-parameter learned position table while BDH uses parameter-free rotary
embeddings; under the non-embedding convention common for Transformers, GPT is
\GptParamsNoEmbK{}K, so BDH is marginally larger instead. Either way the gap is
under \MatchPct{}, far smaller than the 2$\times$ gap being controlled for.
Every other hyperparameter is shared: embedding width $C=128$, 4 heads, 4
layers, context length 128, dropout 0.1.

\paragraph{Training.} Both models are trained from scratch with one shared loop:
AdamW (learning rate $3\times10^{-4}$, weight decay 0.1), batch size 32, context
128, \NIters{} iterations, identical random seed, on the same character-level
TinyShakespeare corpus (a single $\sim$1\,MB file, 65-character vocabulary,
90/10 train/val split). The best-validation checkpoint of each run is kept. The
compute device is an Apple M-series GPU (PyTorch MPS); each model trains in
minutes.

\paragraph{Evaluation.} These are \emph{base} (completion) models, never taught
to answer questions, so ``answer correctness'' is not a valid metric; every
measure below scores how well each model continues Shakespeare-style text. The
harness applies three independent metric families over both checkpoints.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Perplexity} on the held-out validation split, $\exp$ of the mean
  next-character cross-entropy, computed over \NWindows{} non-overlapping
  windows so the whole distribution is visible, not just the mean. Lower is
  better.
\item \textbf{Automatic text-quality} metrics on generated completions, pure
  counting with no model in the loop: fraction of output tokens that are real
  English words (a 235k-word system dictionary plus a short archaic allowlist),
  distinct-2 (bigram diversity), repeat fraction, count of \texttt{NAME:}
  speaker labels, and mean word length. Both models generate from the same
  \NPrompts{} prompts with identical decoding (temperature 0.8, top-$k$ 40, 140
  new tokens) and the same per-prompt seed, so the only variable is the model.
\item \textbf{LLM judge, position-bias controlled.} For each prompt both
  completions are scored by the \texttt{claude} CLI on fluency, coherence, and
  Shakespeare-likeness (1--5 each), and the judge is asked \emph{twice with the
  two completions swapped} ($A\leftrightarrow B$). A win counts as
  \emph{decisive} only if the same model wins in both orderings; a disagreement
  between orderings is logged as ``inconsistent'' and counts for neither. A
  two-sided binomial test is then run on the decisive wins. This directly
  measures and controls for the judge's position bias rather than assuming it
  away.
\end{enumerate}

The prompt set is \NPrompts{} items spanning famous openings (``Now is the
winter of our discontent''), speaker cues (``MACBETH:''), scene cues (``Enter
GHOST.''), and neutral non-Shakespeare English (``The meaning of life is''), the
last as an out-of-distribution stress test. Every number and table in this paper
is emitted by the analysis script from the raw response files.

\subsection{Does the two-times-size win survive matching?}

Yes, essentially intact (Figure~\ref{fig:survive}). The earlier
\BdhBigParamsM{}M-parameter BDH swept the judge \JudgeBigScore{}
($p=\PvalBig$) with perplexity \BdhPplBig{} vs \GptPplBig{}. After removing the
size advantage, the matched \BdhParamsK{}K BDH still sweeps \MatchScore{}
($p=\Pval$) with perplexity \BdhPpl{} vs \GptPpl{}. The two regimes are almost
indistinguishable on the objective metrics, which is the whole point: if the
2$\times$ result had been a pure size effect, shrinking BDH to parity should have
erased it, and it did not. The most telling number is BDH's own perplexity across
the two sizes, \BdhPplBig{} at \BdhBigParamsM{}M and \BdhPpl{} at
\BdhParamsK{}K: cutting its parameters in half cost it essentially nothing, so at
2$\times$ it was carrying redundant capacity, and its edge over the Transformer
was never about being larger.

\subsection{Interpretability: activation sparsity}

BDH's central interpretability claim is that its neurons are positive and sparse
(few fire at once). Measured on the matched, trained model over the validation
set (Figure~\ref{fig:sparsity}), \SparsityFrac{} of neuron activations are active
(the rest sit at exactly zero). That is genuinely sparse, a dense random ReLU
layer sits near 50\% active,
but well above the paper's headline $\sim$5\%, which is expected: that figure
emerges at 10M--1B parameters and long training, while this model is under
1M parameters on 1\,MB of text. It is an honest partial reproduction: the
qualitative property (strong sparsity from the ReLU/positivity design) holds; the
quantitative level does not match the large-scale regime.

\section{Limitations}

This is a small-scale study and its scope should not be overstated. All results
are from one 1\,MB character-level corpus, one embedding width, one depth, and
one training budget; a different scale could reorder the models. Character-level
Shakespeare is a narrow domain, and the automatic word metric rewards
memorized-looking short words. The LLM judge is a single model's opinion; the
swap control removes position bias but not the judge's own stylistic
preferences, though two objective metrics (perplexity and real-word rate) are
reported alongside precisely so the verdict does not rest on the judge alone.
The binomial test also treats prompts as independent trials, whereas a single
judge introduces correlation through its own stylistic prior, so the reported
$p$-values are best read as a strong but optimistic significance bound rather
than an exact one.
Parameter matching equalizes total trainable parameters, not training FLOPs or
wall-clock: BDH's linear attention and depth-wise weight sharing give it a
different compute-per-parameter profile, so ``matched parameters'' is one fair
axis of several. Finally, BDH's perfect-format assistant behavior after
fine-tuning (not evaluated here) is a separate question from base-model
completion quality.

\section{Conclusion}

Built from scratch and trained identically on character-level TinyShakespeare, a
parameter-matched BDH (\BdhParamsK{}K) beats a GPT-2-style Transformer
(\GptParamsK{}K) on every axis measured: lower perplexity (\BdhPpl{} vs
\GptPpl{}), more real words (\BdhRealWord{} vs \GptRealWord{}), lower validation
loss, and a \MatchScore{} sweep of a position-controlled judge ($p=\Pval$). The
result is notable less for the win itself than for what it survives: at its
natural 2$\times$ size BDH sweeps the judge \JudgeBigScore{}, a result that a
size effect alone could fully explain.
Only after equalizing parameters and retraining does the comparison isolate
architecture from scale, and here the architecture holds up. The practical
takeaway is small but sturdy: before crediting a new architecture for beating a
Transformer, match the parameters and retrain; it is a cheap rerun that is the
difference between a real result and a confounded one. All claims here are
bounded to this scale and task, and the harness to test them at another scale is
released with the paper.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.} The released harness contains both model
implementations, the shared training loop, both trained checkpoints, all
generated completions, both orderings of every judge verdict for both the
matched and the two-times-size runs, and the single script that emits every
number, table, and figure in this paper. Retraining is approximately (not
bitwise) reproducible because MPS kernels are non-deterministic; the exact
checkpoints behind these numbers are therefore included.
